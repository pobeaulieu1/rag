# The History and Science of Black Holes

## What Is a Black Hole?

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing — not even light or other electromagnetic waves — has enough speed to escape it. The boundary of no return is called the event horizon. Although crossing the event horizon has enormous consequences for the fate of the infalling object, it has no locally detectable features according to general relativity.

## Formation

Black holes of stellar mass are expected to form when massive stars collapse at the end of their life cycles. After a black hole has formed, it can grow by absorbing mass from its surroundings. Supermassive black holes of millions of solar masses may form by absorbing other stars and merging with other black holes, or via direct collapse of gas clouds.

## Hawking Radiation

In 1974, Stephen Hawking theorized that black holes are not entirely black but instead emit small amounts of thermal radiation due to quantum effects near the event horizon. This radiation, now called Hawking radiation, implies that black holes slowly lose mass over time. For stellar-mass black holes, this process is so slow that it is effectively negligible compared to the mass they absorb from the cosmic microwave background. However, as a black hole loses mass, the Hawking radiation rate increases, potentially ending in an explosion.

## Observational Evidence

The first direct image of a black hole's shadow was captured in 2019 by the Event Horizon Telescope collaboration. The target was the supermassive black hole at the center of the galaxy Messier 87 (M87), which has a mass of approximately 6.5 billion solar masses. A second image, of Sagittarius A* — the black hole at the center of the Milky Way — was released in 2022.

## Spaghettification

Objects falling into a black hole experience tidal forces: the gravitational pull on the side of the object closer to the black hole is stronger than on the far side. For stellar-mass black holes, these forces are strong enough near the event horizon to stretch and compress infalling matter into thin strands — a process called spaghettification. For supermassive black holes, the tidal forces at the event horizon are actually weaker, so an astronaut would not feel spaghettification until well inside the event horizon.

## Time Dilation

General relativity predicts that clocks run slower in stronger gravitational fields. An observer far from a black hole watching an object fall toward it would see the object slow down and appear to freeze asymptotically at the event horizon, becoming increasingly redshifted. From the infalling object's own frame of reference, however, it crosses the event horizon in finite proper time.

## The Information Paradox

One of the deepest unsolved problems in physics is the black hole information paradox. Quantum mechanics requires that information about a physical system is never lost, yet Hawking's calculation suggested that a black hole destroys information as it evaporates. Proposed resolutions include the holographic principle, black hole complementarity, and firewall hypotheses, but none is fully accepted.
